02 — Delivery

From Quarterly Releases to Continuous Delivery at a B2B SaaS

B2B SaaS · Early-stage platform · 7-person team

CI/CD delivery architecture automation

Unlike most engagements, the primary levers here were processes and practices - not deep technical rework. The codebase itself wasn't the bottleneck; the way the team worked was.

The Challenge

Symptoms

A B2B SaaS provider was releasing once every few months, with each release preceded by a multi-week development freeze dedicated entirely to testing. Bugs took weeks to reach users — after being queued for the next release window. The system had roughly 60 independent components, with a roadmap that planned to double that number, all maintained by a team of seven.

Root Cause

Features were developed on long-lived branches, sometimes for weeks or months, before being integrated. By then, the merge cost was high and the integration risk unpredictable. Deployments were largely manual and error-prone. SOC2 compliance on the production environment added further friction — any change to production felt like a risk, so the team avoided it. There was also just one customer onboarded, and they weren't paying yet.

The Approach

  • Black-box test safety net: Before touching the architecture, the team built a suite of black-box tests covering observable system behavior — agnostic to internals. Slow, but comprehensive enough to provide the confidence needed to restructure things underneath.
  • Trunk-based development: Abandoned git-flow entirely. As soon as a chunk of work was stable, it was merged — preventing long-lived branches from drifting, eliminating integration risk, and making the state of the codebase predictable at all times. This single change had the largest downstream impact of the engagement.
  • Shared kernel extraction: Common libraries embedded across multiple components were extracted into standalone packages with their own versioning and release lifecycle — breaking implicit coupling between services.
  • GitHub Actions automation: Manual tagging and deployment processes were replaced with GitHub Actions pipelines, enabling components to be versioned and deployed independently without human coordination.
  • Independent deployability: With automation and decoupled dependencies in place, components could be deployed without touching the rest of the system — drastically reducing blast radius and making rollbacks tractable.
  • Separate non-SOC2 environment: A dedicated production-like environment outside the SOC2 compliance boundary was introduced. The team could now experiment, validate assumptions, and iterate freely — without the psychological weight of touching regulated infrastructure. Fear of deployment evaporated.

The Outcome

What had been a quarterly release cycle became multiple deploys per day. The pre-release testing freeze disappeared — replaced by automated safety nets and incremental integration. Each deployment became a small, targeted change rather than a high-stakes batch release. MTTR dropped significantly as bugs could be isolated, fixed, and shipped in hours. The team started shipping with confidence rather than caution.
